## Experiment 8 Summary: Depth × Compression Interaction

**Novel insight:** Both depth and compression force networks toward nonlinear encoding through different mechanisms. You can trade off one for the other—if you have high compression, you need less depth (and vice versa). This provides flexible design choices for practitioners.

- Maximum nonlinear gain scales with n: 0.0017 (n=32) → 0.0087 (n=128)

## Experiment 15 Summary: Residual Connections vs Standard Autoencoders

**Key Finding**: Counter-intuitively, residual/skip connections make nonlinear encoding **worse**, not better.

**Results**:
- Standard autoencoders outperformed residual autoencoders at ALL tested depths (l=4,6,8,10,12)
- At l=4, standard achieved gain=0.0045 vs residual's gain=0.0002 (94.6% worse)
- At l≥8, standard collapsed to exactly linear (gain=0), but residual was even worse with **negative** gains (-0.002 to -0.004)
- Residual networks achieved better raw MSE, but this came at the cost of nonlinear encoding

**Why Residual Fails for This Task**:
- Skip connections (x + f(x)) bias toward identity/linear behavior
- For bottleneck autoencoders, we **want** the network to be forced into nonlinear compression
- Skip connections provide an "escape route" that reduces the pressure to learn nonlinear representations
- This is fundamentally different from classification tasks where residuals help

**Key Insight**: The depth ceiling at l≈7-8 is NOT purely a vanishing gradient problem. If it were, residual connections would have helped. The ceiling appears to be related to the optimization landscape or the bottleneck constraint itself.

**Suggested Next Steps** (from the log):
1. Test layer/batch normalization WITHOUT skip connections
2. Investigate if bottleneck width (m) affects the depth ceiling
3. Test different initialization schemes
4. Try different optimizers (SGD+momentum, AdamW)

## Experiment 16 Summary: Normalization to Push the Depth Ceiling

**Hypothesis**: Layer/batch normalization (without skip connections) would stabilize training at extreme depths (l≥8) and enable positive nonlinear encoding beyond the depth ceiling discovered in Experiment 14.

**Key Findings**:

1. **Normalization PARTIALLY overcomes the depth ceiling**: LayerNorm achieves positive nonlinear gain at l=8 and l=10 (mean=0.0003), while standard networks collapse to exactly zero. However, this is a ~15× reduction compared to peak performance at l=4.

2. **But normalization HURTS moderate-depth performance**: At l=4-6, standard networks achieve gain=0.0037-0.0045, while LayerNorm achieves only -0.0002 to 0.0010. Normalization interferes with learning nonlinear representations where standard networks already excel.

3. **LayerNorm > BatchNorm**: LayerNorm consistently outperforms BatchNorm at all tested depths. BatchNorm often produces negative nonlinear gains.

4. **Fundamental tradeoff discovered**: You can either have (a) high nonlinear gain at moderate depth (standard networks) or (b) any nonlinear gain at extreme depth (normalized networks), but not both.

5. **The depth ceiling is NOT purely a gradient problem**: Normalization stabilizes gradients (depth-gain correlation becomes positive) yet doesn't restore high performance. This suggests the ceiling relates to representational capacity or optimization landscape.

**Practical recommendation**: Use standard (unnormalized) networks at l=4-6 for maximum nonlinear encoding. Only use LayerNorm if extreme depth (l≥8) is required and ~10× lower nonlinear gain is acceptable.

**Suggested next experiments**:
1. Test whether larger bottleneck width (m) allows deeper networks without normalization
2. Test different optimizers (SGD+momentum, AdamW) 
3. Test whether CR=32 allows deeper networks than CR=16

## Experiment 19: Optimizer Effects on Depth Ceiling

**Key Finding:** No optimizer tested (Adam, AdamW, SGD+momentum, Adam+warmup) could break through the depth ceiling at l≈6-7. This confirms the ceiling is NOT optimizer-specific—it's a fundamental property of the bottleneck autoencoder architecture.

**Results:**
- **AdamW performed best** at moderate depths (max gain 0.00508 at l=6), slightly outperforming vanilla Adam
- **SGD+momentum completely failed** at ALL depths, achieving ~0 nonlinear gain and much higher MSE (0.035 vs 0.028)
- **Adam+warmup** showed similar performance to vanilla Adam, with no breakthrough at l≥8
- **All optimizers collapsed to exactly zero nonlinear gain at l≥8**, even with gradient clipping enabled

**Implication:** The depth ceiling is intrinsic to the architecture, not the optimizer. To train deeper networks that maintain nonlinear encoding, entirely different approaches may be needed (progressive depth training, auxiliary losses, or different architectures altogether).

**Suggested next experiments:**
1. Progressive depth training (train shallow first, then gradually add layers)
2. Auxiliary reconstruction losses at intermediate layers
